The Art and History of Sarees

Discover the fascinating world of traditional Indian sarees, their historical significance, and the intricate craftsmanship behind these timeless garments.

Types of Sarees Across India

Banarasi Sarees

Originating from Varanasi (formerly Banaras), these sarees are known for their gold and silver brocade or zari work. The intricate designs often feature floral, foliate patterns, and the use of meenakari (colorful enamel work).

These sarees hold special significance in Indian weddings and are often passed down as heirlooms through generations. The creation of a single Banarasi saree can take anywhere from 15 days to 6 months depending on the complexity of design.

Kanjivaram Sarees

Hailing from Kanchipuram in Tamil Nadu, these silk sarees are characterized by their bright colors, heavy gold zari work, and contrasting borders. Traditional motifs include temple designs, checks, stripes, and floral patterns.

Kanjivaram sarees are woven using pure mulberry silk threads. The body and border of the saree are woven separately and then interlocked, a technique known as 'korvai'.

The Weaving Process

The creation of a handloom saree involves several intricate steps:

  1. Yarn Processing: The silk or cotton threads are prepared, dyed, and made ready for weaving.
  2. Designing: Artisans create patterns either freehand or using graph paper.
  3. Warping: The vertical threads (warp) are arranged on the loom.
  4. Weft Insertion: The horizontal threads (weft) are interlaced with the warp to create the fabric.
  5. Special Techniques: Depending on the saree type, techniques like jacquard, jamdani, or zari work are employed.
  6. Finishing: The saree is washed, pressed, and made ready for sale.

This meticulous process can take anywhere from a few days to several months, depending on the complexity of the design and techniques used.

Preserving a Heritage

Traditional handloom saree weaving is facing challenges from power looms and machine-made textiles. At 11Sari, we're committed to preserving this artform by:

  • Working directly with master weavers and their families
  • Providing fair compensation for their craftsmanship
  • Documenting traditional techniques for future generations
  • Creating opportunities for young artisans to learn the craft
  • Promoting awareness about the value of handloom sarees

By choosing handloom sarees, you're not just acquiring a beautiful garment—you're supporting a centuries-old tradition and the artisans who keep it alive.
